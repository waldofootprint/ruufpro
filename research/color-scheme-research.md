# Color Scheme Research: Roofing Contractor Template Websites

Research into optimal color palettes for small residential roofing contractor websites. Target feel: **trustworthy, high quality, local, professional.**

Constraints: white/light backgrounds, DM Serif Display + DM Sans fonts, template system where contractors can eventually pick a palette.

---

## 1. What Colors Do Top Roofing Websites Use?

### Industry-Wide Patterns

The roofing and home services industry overwhelmingly gravitates toward a narrow band of colors:

**Most common primary colors (in order of prevalence):**

1. **Dark blue / navy** — By far the #1 choice. Used by the majority of established roofing companies. Conveys trust, stability, professionalism. Examples: GAF-certified contractor sites, large regional roofers, Roofr's own branding.
2. **Dark red / maroon** — Second most common. Communicates strength, urgency, and craftsmanship. Pairs well with dark neutrals.
3. **Forest green / dark green** — Growing trend, especially for companies emphasizing sustainability, energy efficiency, or eco-friendly materials.
4. **Charcoal / dark gray** — Used as a primary by premium/luxury-positioned roofers. Modern, clean, high-end feel.
5. **Orange / burnt orange** — Less common as primary but very popular as an accent/CTA color. High energy, stands out against blue.

**Most common accent colors:**

- **Orange** (#E87C2A range) — The single most effective CTA color in home services. High contrast against blue primaries, conveys urgency and energy.
- **Yellow / gold** (#F5A623 range) — Warmth, optimism, attention-grabbing. Common for "Get a Quote" buttons.
- **Bright red** (#D32F2F range) — Urgency. Used sparingly for CTAs on sites with non-red primaries.
- **Green** (#2E7D32 range) — Used for trust signals, checkmarks, "verified" badges.
- **White** — Clean CTAs on dark backgrounds (dark hero with white "Get Free Estimate" button).

**Background patterns:**

- White primary background with light gray (#F5F5F5 to #F8FAFC) alternating sections is nearly universal
- Very light blue tint (#F0F7FF) for trust-building sections (testimonials, certifications)
- Dark sections (navy/charcoal) used sparingly for hero, footer, or a single mid-page CTA band

### Why These Colors Dominate

Roofing is a high-trust, high-cost purchase. Homeowners are spending $8,000-$25,000+ and inviting strangers onto their property. The color choices reflect what matters to the buyer:
- **I won't get scammed** (blue = trust)
- **This company is established** (dark/muted tones = maturity)
- **They do quality work** (clean design = attention to detail)
- **They're responsive** (orange/warm accents = energy, action)

## 2. Color Psychology for Home Services

### Colors That Build Trust

| Color | Psychology | Home Services Application |
|-------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Navy / Dark Blue** (#1B3A5C) | Trust, dependability, competence, calm authority | Primary brand color. Hero backgrounds, navigation, headings. Blue is the #1 "trust color" across all industries — banks, insurance, and contractors all lean on it. |
| **Slate / Steel Blue** (#475569) | Sophistication, reliability, modern professionalism | A more contemporary alternative to navy. Works for contractors targeting newer/upscale homes. |
| **Forest Green** (#1B5E20) | Growth, stability, safety, environmental responsibility | Strong for companies emphasizing energy-efficient roofing, sustainability, or "protecting your home." |
| **Warm Gray / Charcoal** (#374151) | Strength, timelessness, premium quality | Works as primary for luxury-positioned contractors. Pairs with gold or warm accents. |
| **Deep Red / Maroon** (#7B1F1F) | Strength, craftsmanship, established heritage | Communicates "we've been doing this for decades." Good for family-owned businesses. |

### Colors That Drive Action (Accents/CTAs)

| Color | Psychology | Best Use |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Orange** (#EA580C) | Energy, enthusiasm, friendly urgency | CTA buttons, phone numbers, "Get Your Free Estimate." Orange consistently outperforms other CTA colors in home services A/B tests. |
| **Amber / Gold** (#D97706) | Optimism, quality, warmth | CTAs, star ratings, premium tier badges. Conveys quality without the urgency of orange. |
| **Bright Blue** (#2563EB) | Confidence, clarity, clickability | Links, secondary CTAs. Users instinctively recognize blue as clickable. |
| **Green** (#16A34A) | Safety, go, verified, savings | "Licensed & Insured" badges, savings callouts, checkmark lists. |

### Colors to Avoid

| Color | Why It Fails for Roofing |
|-------|------------------------|
| **Purple** | Associated with luxury/beauty/creative industries. Feels out of place for a trades business. Homeowners may subconsciously question legitimacy. |
| **Pink / Magenta** | Same issue — disconnected from the home services mental model. |
| **Neon / Electric colors** | Cheap, untrustworthy, aggressive. The opposite of what a $15K purchase needs. |
| **All-black themes** | Too stark, impersonal. Can feel corporate or intimidating for a local business. |
| **Pastel primaries** | Too soft. Roofing needs to convey strength and durability. Pastels work for daycare websites, not contractors. |
| **Too many colors** | More than 2-3 colors looks disorganized. Contractors need to look buttoned-up. |

### The Trust Formula for Home Services

The research consensus across marketing studies and conversion optimization data points to this hierarchy:

1. **Dark, muted primary** (navy, charcoal, forest green, or maroon) = "We're established and professional"
2. **Warm, energetic accent** (orange, amber, or gold) = "We're responsive and easy to work with"
3. **Clean white/light gray backgrounds** = "We're organized and detail-oriented"
4. **Minimal palette (2-3 colors max)** = "We're focused and trustworthy"

All four palettes are designed to work with DM Serif Display (headings) + DM Sans (body), white/light backgrounds, and a "trustworthy, high quality, local" feel.

## 5. Dark Hero Sections

### Are Dark Hero Sections Effective for Contractor Sites?

**Yes, overwhelmingly.** Dark hero sections are the dominant pattern in high-converting roofing websites. Here is why:

1. **Photo overlay:** Roofing hero images (houses, roofs, crews working) need a dark overlay to make text readable. This naturally creates a dark hero.
2. **Contrast for CTAs:** A bright orange or white CTA button pops dramatically against a dark hero, driving clicks.
3. **Authority signal:** A dark hero section immediately communicates "established, serious business" before the visitor reads a single word.
4. **Visual hierarchy:** Dark-to-light transition (dark hero flowing into white content) creates a natural reading flow.

### What Works Best for Dark Heroes

**Navy hero (`#1E3A5F` to `#0F2440`):**
- The safest choice. Universally trustworthy.
- Pairs with: white text, orange CTA, light blue secondary CTA
- Best overlay: semi-transparent navy over a high-quality roof/home photo

**Charcoal hero (`#1F2937` to `#111827`):**
- More modern, premium feel
- Pairs with: white text, gold or amber CTA
- Best overlay: gradient from solid dark-left to transparent-right, revealing the photo

**Slate hero (`#334155` to `#1E293B`):**
- Warmer than pure charcoal, less expected than navy
- Pairs with: white text, amber or orange CTA
- Best overlay: even semi-transparent layer

**Deep teal hero (`#134E4A` to `#0D3330`):**
- Distinctive, modern
- Pairs with: white text, coral or warm orange CTA
- Best overlay: semi-transparent with slight gradient

### Dark Hero Best Practices

- **Always use a photo underneath** — even with a heavy overlay, the texture of a real photo adds depth and authenticity vs. a flat color block
- **Overlay opacity: 60-80%** — enough to ensure text readability (WCAG AA contrast), light enough to show the photo
- **Keep hero text minimal:** Heading (DM Serif Display, white, large), one-line subheading (DM Sans, white/light gray), one or two CTA buttons
- **CTA button colors on dark heroes:** Primary CTA in accent color (orange/gold/coral), secondary CTA as white outline (ghost button)
- **Avoid pure black (#000000)** as a hero background — it feels like a loading error. Always use a tinted dark color.

### Dark Hero + Light Body Transition

The transition from dark hero to white content body is critical:

- **Hard edge** (most common): Dark hero ends, white section begins. Clean and professional. Works best with a slight shadow or a colored accent bar between sections.
- **Slight curve/wave:** A subtle SVG wave divider from dark to white. Modern but can look gimmicky if overdone.
- **Angled cut:** A diagonal divider. Dynamic but can clash with the conservative feel needed for trust.

**Recommendation:** Hard edge with a thin accent-colored stripe (2-4px of the accent color) at the bottom of the hero, then straight into white content. Clean, professional, and intentional.

## 6. Recommendation for RoofReady Default

**Palette A ("Classic Contractor" — Navy + Orange) should be the default template** for these reasons:

1. It is the most universally effective color scheme in roofing
2. It works for every type of residential roofer regardless of market or positioning
3. It has the lowest risk of turning off any homeowner demographic
4. DM Serif Display in navy blue looks authoritative and premium
5. Orange CTAs are proven high-converters in home services
6. The current brand variables in `globals.css` are already in the blue family (`#1E3A5F` is already defined as brand-900)

The other palettes (B, C, D) should be available as customization options in the template system, giving contractors a way to differentiate without needing design skills.

### Implementation Note

The current `globals.css` uses CSS custom properties (Tailwind v4 `@theme`) which makes palette swapping straightforward. Each palette would just be a different set of `--color-brand-*` values, potentially with a `--color-accent-*` scale added for the CTA/action colors.
