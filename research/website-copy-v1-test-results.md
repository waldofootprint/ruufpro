# RuufPro Website Copy V1 — Test Results

Ruthless critique of `/research/website-copy-v1.md` against seven tests. Every problem is quoted, diagnosed, and rewritten where needed.

---

## TEST 1: HORMOZI VALUE EQUATION

The Value Equation: Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)

### Dream Outcome: B+

The copy does a decent job pointing at outcomes — "makes your phone ring," "stop losing jobs," "leads come to you while you're on the roof." But it hedges too often. The hero headline is the biggest miss:

> "Homeowners are searching for a roofer right now. Are they finding you?"

This is a QUESTION, not a promise. It asks the roofer to self-diagnose rather than painting the outcome. Compare to Roofle's "Sell Roofs In Your Sleep" — that IS the dream outcome in five words. Your hero asks a question; it should deliver a vision.

**Section-by-section grades:**

| Section | Grade | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Hero | B- | Question headline lacks punch. Subhead does the heavy lifting. |
| Urgency (Google Problem) | A- | Strong fear-based outcome. "You lose jobs you never knew existed" is excellent. |
| Cost of Inaction | B | The comparison table is features, not outcomes. The headline is the best line on the page. |
| The Solution | B+ | "Makes your phone ring. Free." is great. But the feature cards immediately revert to feature-speak. |
| Proof | C | "See what your website looks like" is not an outcome. Nobody cares what the site looks like — they care if it works. |
| How It Works | B+ | Clean and fast. The time markers (30 seconds, 15 seconds, Instant) are smart. |
| Anti-Agency | A- | Strong emotional resonance. Addresses the burned-by-agencies feeling directly. |
| Upgrade Path | B+ | "Warmer leads. Fewer tire-kickers." is outcome language. Good. |
| Pricing | B | Standard feature lists. Not outcome-framed. |
| FAQ | B+ | Handles objections well but misses chances to re-sell outcomes. |
| Final CTA | B- | "Your competitors have websites. In 2 minutes, you will too." — This sells PARITY, not advantage. Having a website isn't the dream. Getting calls is the dream. |

**Rewrite — Hero headline:**
> Your phone should be ringing more. We'll make that happen — free.

Or steal from your own research doc (marketing-copy-research.md, Option A):
> Stop losing jobs to roofers with better websites.

Both are stronger because they name the outcome or the pain, not a question.

**Rewrite — Final CTA headline:**
> In 2 minutes, homeowners start finding you instead of your competitor.

### Perceived Likelihood: D+

This is the biggest hole in the entire page. There is ZERO proof. No testimonials. No case studies. No numbers from real users. No screenshots of real leads. No "roofer X got Y leads in Z days."

The marketing-copy-research.md file has placeholder testimonials (Mike R., Carlos M., Jeff T.) that sound authentic. They are not on this page. Even if you can't use real testimonials yet, the page has literally nothing between "trust us" and "give us your info."

The "See It Live" section (Section 5) tries to fill this gap with a demo site, but:

> "This isn't a mockup. Click through a real RuufPro site and see the quality for yourself."

"See the quality for yourself" is about the PRODUCT, not the RESULT. A roofer doesn't care if the site is pretty. They care if it generates calls.

**What's missing:**
- At minimum, a live demo site with a working estimate widget
- The $187/lead Google Ads stat from your pain points research — this is gold and it's nowhere on the page
- The "2/3 of roofers are unhappy with their marketing provider" stat is used but not sourced visibly
- No before/after story, even a hypothetical one

**Recommendation:** Until you have real testimonials, lean HARD on verifiable industry stats. The $187/lead cost, the 78% wanting pricing, the 63% saying lead gen is their #1 challenge — these build credibility through third-party data. Stack them. Make a "the numbers don't lie" section.

### Time Delay: A-

This is your strongest dimension. "2 minutes" is repeated throughout and the How It Works section breaks it down with specific time markers (30 seconds, 15 seconds, Instant). The trust line hammers it: "Free forever. No credit card. Live in 2 minutes."

**One concern:** The copy says "2 minutes" but the marketing-copy-research.md says "5 minutes" everywhere. Pick one and be consistent. If it's really 2 minutes, say 2. If you're rounding down aggressively, say "under 5 minutes" — which is still fast and more believable.

### Effort & Sacrifice: A-

"No tech skills. No credit card. No catch." is clean. The How It Works section makes it feel effortless. "Three questions. Two minutes. You're live." is one of the best lines on the page.

**Minor issue:** The copy says "Three questions" but then lists business name, phone, city, AND a design style choice. That's four things, or arguably two steps. Be precise — roofers notice when you fudge numbers.

## TEST 2: AUDIENCE FIT

### Would a busy roofer on their phone actually read this? MOSTLY YES, with exceptions.

The page is generally well-structured for scanning. Short sentences, bold callouts, clear sections. But several sections are too long for a phone screen:

**Section 2 (Urgency)** — The body paragraph is 4 sentences that could be 2:

> "In December 2025, Google added an 'Online Estimates' filter to search. When homeowners click it — and 1 in 5 already do — roofers without online pricing disappear from results completely. It doesn't matter how many reviews you have or how long you've been in business. No online estimates? No visibility."

The first two sentences do the job. The last two are belt-and-suspenders. Cut to:

> "Google added an 'Online Estimates' filter. 1 in 5 homeowners already use it. If you don't offer online pricing, you disappear. Doesn't matter how many reviews you have."

**Section 3 (Cost of Inaction)** — The comparison table is excellent for a roofer scanning on their phone. Tables work. Keep it.

**Section 7 (Anti-Agency)** — The bullet list is perfect roofer reading. Short, punchy, no BS. This is the best-written section on the page for audience fit.

### Too corporate or SaaS-y?

A few lines cross into SaaS territory:

- **"Lead Dashboard (Included)"** — "Dashboard" is SaaS-speak. Roofers think in terms of "seeing my leads" not "accessing my dashboard." Rewrite: "Your Leads, One Place" or "Never Miss a Lead"
- **"SEO Built In (Automatic)"** — Many roofers don't know what SEO means. This needs a translation. Better: "Show Up on Google (Automatic)"
- **"Meta tags, schema markup, and local optimization"** — A roofer's eyes glaze over at "schema markup." Rewrite: "All the behind-the-scenes Google stuff — handled for you."
- **"yourname.ruufpro.com subdomain"** — "Subdomain" is tech jargon. Say "your own web address" or just show the example without labeling it.

### Does it sound like someone who understands roofers?

Mostly yes. Lines like "Leads come to you while you're on the roof" and "You're great at roofing" show empathy. But the page never once mentions:
- Being on a roof in 100-degree heat and getting a lead notification
- The frustration of losing a job to a guy with a nicer truck and a website
- The reality that most roofers got into this because they're good with their hands, not with computers
- Storm season, insurance work, seasonal swings — none of the REAL roofer context

The marketing-copy-research.md nailed this: "A great website isn't just about leads — it's about self-respect and pride." That emotional truth is absent from the V1 copy.

## TEST 3: OBJECTION HANDLING

### Every objection a skeptical roofer would have:

| # | Objection | Addressed? | Where? | Grade |
|---|-----------|-----------|--------|-------|
| 1 | "If it's free, what's the catch?" | Yes | FAQ + trust line + Section 7 | A |
| 2 | "I'm not a tech guy" | Yes | FAQ + How It Works | A- |
| 3 | "My buddy got burned by a marketing company" | Yes | Section 7 (Anti-Agency) | A |
| 4 | "I already have a website" | Yes | FAQ | B+ |
| 5 | "How do I know the estimates are accurate?" | Partially | FAQ mentions "ballpark" but no accuracy data | C |
| 6 | "Will this actually show up on Google?" | Weakly | SEO section mentions it but no proof | C- |
| 7 | "Who are you people?" | NO | Zero information about the company/founder | F |
| 8 | "What happens to my site if you go out of business?" | NO | Not addressed anywhere | F |
| 9 | "How many roofers are using this?" | NO | No social proof, no user count | D |
| 10 | "Can I see a real site that's gotten real leads?" | NO | Demo exists but no results shown | D |
| 11 | "$99/mo adds up — is it worth it?" | Partially | Compared to Roofle but no ROI framing | C |
| 12 | "I don't want homeowners lowballing me with online prices" | NO | This is a HUGE unaddressed objection | F |
| 13 | "What if my competitor in my city is also on RuufPro?" | NO | Not addressed | F |

### Critical missing objections:

**#7 — "Who are you people?"** This is fatal for trust. There is zero "About" content on the page. No founder story. No "built by" statement. For a free product asking roofers to hand over their business info, they need to know WHO is behind this. Even one sentence: "Built by [name], who watched her [family member] lose jobs to competitors with better websites" — something human.

**#12 — "I don't want homeowners lowballing me."** Every roofer who's been in the business more than a year has a horror story about a homeowner who got an estimate from somewhere and then argued the price down. Online estimates trigger this fear. You MUST address it. The FAQ should include:

> **Q: Won't online estimates just give homeowners ammunition to haggle?**
> A: You control the pricing. These are YOUR numbers, not ours. And a homeowner who's already seen your price and still fills out the form is a warmer lead than someone who's just shopping around. The estimate qualifies them before you ever pick up the phone.

**#13 — "What if my competitor uses this too?"** If two roofers in the same city both have RuufPro sites, what happens? This will come up immediately in any small market. You need an answer, even if it's "there's room for everyone" or "we limit sites per market."

### Where would they stop reading?

- **Section 5 (Proof)** — "See what your website looks like" is the weakest section. If the demo is a generic template, a roofer will think "that's not MY business" and bounce. If the demo is impressive, it saves this section. The copy alone doesn't carry it.
- **The comparison table** — If a roofer doesn't know who Roofle is (and many won't), the table loses impact. Consider leading with the AGENCY comparison (which every roofer relates to) instead of the Roofle comparison.

### Where would they lose trust?

- **The 1 in 5 stat without visible sourcing.** Roofers are skeptical people. If they Google "1 in 5 homeowners use online estimates filter" and can't verify it, trust drops.
- **"Free forever" with no explanation of WHY it's free until Section 7.** That's too late. A skeptical roofer will bounce before Section 7. Move the business model explanation MUCH earlier — ideally right after the hero. "Why is it free? Because we make money when you upgrade to Pro. If you never do, the free site is still yours. That's it."

## TEST 4: COMPETITIVE POSITIONING

### vs. Roofle ($350/mo + $2K setup): B+

The differentiation is clear and specific:

> "Roofle charges $350/mo plus a $2,000 setup fee — that's $6,200 in year one — for an estimate widget only. No website included."

This is strong. Dollar-specific, factual, and highlights the website gap. But two issues:

1. **"71% less than Roofle"** — This math is misleading. $99 is 71% less than $350 on a monthly basis, but Roofle's $350 includes features RuufPro doesn't offer (unlimited proposals, material ordering, financing integrations, Owens Corning partnership). You're comparing monthly prices for different products. A more honest framing: "Our estimate widget does the same core job for $99/mo vs. their $350/mo."

2. **Many roofers have never heard of Roofle.** You're spending significant page real estate positioning against a competitor your audience may not know. Lead with the problem ("online estimates are expensive"), then use Roofle as one data point.

### vs. Agencies ($3-5K): A-

This is where the copy sings, because every roofer has either been burned by an agency or knows someone who has. Section 7 (Anti-Agency) is the most emotionally resonant section on the page. The bullet points are perfect:

> "No salesperson. Sign up yourself in 2 minutes."
> "No contract. Cancel anytime. Your site stays live."

One miss: The copy says "Your site stays live" after canceling. Is this true for the free tier only, or also for Pro? Clarify — this is a trust-building detail that matters.

### vs. DIY (Wix/Squarespace): B

The comparison is fair but undersells the real pain of DIY. The table says "You build it" and "40+ hours." But the real objection to Wix isn't time — it's that the result looks amateur. A roofer who spends 40 hours on Wix will have a site that screams "I built this myself." The copy should say that explicitly:

> "You could spend a weekend on Wix. But a homeowner can tell the difference between a Wix template and a professional site — and they'll call the roofer who looks like they have their act together."

### Is the comparison fair and honest? MOSTLY.

The comparison table has one cherry-pick that a sharp competitor could call out:

> "Setup fee: $0 | $2,000 | $3,000+ | $0"

Roofle's annual plan ($5,500/yr) includes setup. The $2,000 setup fee only applies to the monthly plan. The copy exclusively uses the most expensive Roofle configuration. This is technically accurate but selectively presented. Consider adding a footnote or using the annual price as well: "Roofle: $350/mo + $2K setup (monthly) or $5,500/yr (annual)."

Being scrupulously honest here actually STRENGTHENS your position — $5,500/yr is still 4.6x your max annual cost.

## TEST 5: CONVERSION FLOW

### Is there a clear single path to conversion? YES, but it's slightly muddled.

The primary CTA ("Build My Free Website") is consistent and clear. The secondary CTA ("See a live example") supports without competing. This is good.

**Problem: There are TWO conversion paths — free website AND $99/mo Pro.** The page tries to sell both simultaneously. Sections 1-7 sell the free website. Section 8 suddenly pivots to selling the upgrade. Section 9 presents both tiers.

For a cold visitor, this creates decision friction. They came for "free website" and now they're evaluating a $99/mo subscription. The page should commit: **the landing page sells the FREE website. Period.** The upgrade sells itself once they're inside the product.

### CTA inventory:

| Location | CTA | Target |
|----------|-----|--------|
| Hero | Build My Free Website | Free signup |
| Hero | See a live example | Demo |
| Section 8 | Add Estimates to My Site — $99/mo | Paid upgrade |
| Section 9 | Get Started Free | Free signup |
| Section 9 | Start Pro — $99/mo | Paid upgrade |
| Section 11 | Build My Free Website | Free signup |

Six CTAs across 12 sections. That's about right for a long-form page. But the two paid CTAs (Section 8, Section 9) create a fork. A roofer who was ready to click "Build My Free Website" now pauses: "Wait, do I need the $99 thing too?"

**Recommendation:** Keep the pricing section for transparency but make the Pro CTA secondary everywhere. The page's job is to get them to create a free account. The product's job is to upsell them.

### Where might someone bounce?

1. **After the hero, if it doesn't load fast.** Mobile roofer on cellular data — this page needs to be light.
2. **At Section 5 (Proof)** if the demo isn't impressive or doesn't load.
3. **At Section 8 (Upgrade Path)** — this is where "free" suddenly has a $99 price tag next to it. If the transition isn't smooth, it feels like a bait-and-switch.
4. **At the FAQ** — if they've scrolled this far without converting, the FAQ is their last chance. The FAQ is solid but could be tighter.

### Flow recommendation:

The current order is: Hero > Urgency > Cost of Inaction > Solution > Proof > How It Works > Anti-Agency > Upgrade > Pricing > FAQ > Final CTA.

Suggested reorder:
1. Hero (hook)
2. Anti-Agency / Trust (immediately explain why it's free — defuse the #1 objection)
3. How It Works (show how easy it is — 3 steps)
4. Proof (demo)
5. Urgency / Google Problem (now that they trust you, create urgency)
6. Solution features (what they get)
7. Comparison table (why this beats alternatives)
8. Pricing (transparent, simple)
9. FAQ
10. Final CTA

Rationale: The current flow leads with FEAR (Google is hiding you!) before TRUST. A skeptical roofer will dismiss the fear as a sales tactic if they don't trust you first. Build trust, then introduce urgency.

## TEST 6: TONE & VOICE

### Consistency: B+

The tone is mostly consistent — direct, practical, slightly conversational. But it shifts in a few places:

**Section 2 (Urgency)** gets dramatic:
> "It doesn't matter how many reviews you have or how long you've been in business. No online estimates? No visibility."

This is punchy and works. But then Section 5 goes soft:
> "This isn't a mockup. Click through a real RuufPro site and see the quality for yourself."

"See the quality for yourself" sounds like a furniture commercial. The tonal shift from "you're invisible on Google" to "look at this nice website" is jarring.

### Condescension check: PASS (mostly)

The copy is respectful. "I'm not a tech guy" is framed as relatable, not deficient. "No tech skills" doesn't imply they're stupid — it implies the product is simple. Good.

**One flag:**
> "We built what should have existed"

This is slightly self-congratulatory. It centers the BUILDER, not the ROOFER. Rewrite: "Finally, a professional roofing website that doesn't cost thousands or take weeks." Same meaning, roofer-centered.

### Too casual or too corporate?

The trust line "We make money by being useful, not by locking you in" is excellent — casual but credible.

The comparison table headers are too corporate: "Roofing-specific" as a feature label means nothing to a roofer scanning fast. Better: "Built for roofers."

### Anti-Agency section: AUTHENTIC

Section 7 reads as genuine. The bullet structure is smart — it anticipates the specific things agencies do wrong and counters each one. The callout "We make money by being useful, not by locking you in" is the best line on the page. It should be more prominent — consider pulling it up to the hero area.

The only risk: a truly skeptical roofer will think "that's exactly what an agency would say." Counter with specificity — show the actual cancellation button, show the actual pricing page, show the actual lead export. Proof beats promises.

## TEST 7: CLAIMS & HONESTY

### Claim audit:

| Claim | Verifiable? | Risk Level |
|-------|------------|------------|
| "Free forever" | Depends on business model sustainability | MEDIUM — if you ever change this, it's a PR disaster. Consider "Free plan available" as safer language, though less punchy. |
| "Live in 2 minutes" | Testable — time your own signup flow | LOW if true. HIGH if it's actually 5-7 minutes. |
| "1 in 5 homeowners use the filter" | Single source (RoofersCoffeeShop) | MEDIUM — your own research doc flags this as thin data |
| "78% want pricing before they call" | Industry stat, multiple sources | LOW |
| "76% of roofers don't show pricing" | Needs sourcing | MEDIUM — where is this from? |
| "$6,200 first year for Roofle" | Verifiable from their pricing page | LOW — but use their current pricing at time of publish |
| "71% cheaper than Roofle" | Math checks out on monthly price only | MEDIUM — misleading if feature sets differ significantly |
| "Satellite roof measurements" | Depends on your API accuracy | LOW if properly caveated as "ballpark" |
| "Qualifies for Google's Online Estimates filter" | Needs testing/verification | HIGH — if Google doesn't actually surface RuufPro widget sites in the filter, this is a broken promise |
| "Your reviews and SEO stop working" (Section 2) | Overstatement | HIGH — the filter doesn't break SEO. It adds a FILTER. Without the filter active, SEO still works normally. This line implies permanent damage. |

### The "1 in 5" stat:

Your own research doc says: "The '1 in 5' stat comes from one source (RoofersCoffeeShop webinar promo). No major study has quantified the exact visibility loss."

The copy uses this stat THREE times (Section 2 body, Section 2 stats row, and FAQ). For a stat from a single webinar promo, this is overexposure. Recommendations:

- Use it once, with attribution: "According to RoofersCoffeeShop, 1 in 5 homeowners are already using this filter."
- Frame it as directional, not definitive: "Early data suggests around 1 in 5 homeowners are using the filter — and that number is growing."
- Do NOT put it in a "stats row" that implies hard research alongside the 78% stat (which is better sourced).

### "Free forever" believability:

Roofers have heard "free" before. The page does a good job explaining the business model (widget revenue), but it comes too late (Section 7). The skeptic has bounced by then.

Additionally, "free forever" is a legal commitment. If you ever need to change the business model, this language creates liability. Consider: "Free. Not a trial. Not '14 days free.' Your website costs nothing — today and going forward." This conveys permanence without a literally infinite legal commitment.

### Specific problem line — Section 2:

> "Your reviews and SEO stop working"

This is false. Reviews and SEO still work for the 80% of homeowners NOT using the filter. When the filter is off, everything works normally. This line implies your SEO is "broken" without online estimates, which is inaccurate and could damage credibility if a tech-savvy roofer or a competitor calls it out.

**Rewrite:**
> "Your reviews and SEO don't help when homeowners filter you out"

### Specific problem line — Section 8:

> "$99/mo — 71% less than Roofle"

As noted above, this compares monthly subscription prices for products with different feature sets. Roofle's $350 includes proposals, material ordering, financing tools, and manufacturer partnerships. RuufPro's $99 is the estimate widget. These aren't equivalent products.

**Safer rewrite:**
> "$99/mo for satellite estimates — vs. $350/mo at Roofle for a similar widget"

### Legal risks:

1. **"Free forever"** — binding language if displayed prominently. Use "free plan" in legal/terms contexts.
2. **Roofle pricing claims** — verify these are current at time of publish. If Roofle changes their pricing, your page becomes inaccurate.
3. **"Qualifies for Google's Online Estimates filter"** — if this can't be verified with certainty, soften to "designed to qualify" or "built to meet Google's requirements."
4. **No "results not typical" disclaimer** — if you ever add testimonials with specific lead/revenue numbers, you'll need one.

## OVERALL ASSESSMENT

### What's working:
- The Anti-Agency section (Section 7) is genuinely strong and emotionally resonant
- The comparison table is clear and effective for scanners
- The How It Works section makes the product feel effortless
- "2 minutes" as a repeated anchor is smart
- The FAQ is comprehensive and written in human language
- The Google filter urgency is a real, defensible, timely angle

### What needs the most work:
1. **Hero headline is weak** — a question instead of a promise. This is the most-read line on the page and it's the least compelling.
2. **Zero social proof** — no testimonials, no case studies, no user count, no logos, nothing. This is the single biggest conversion killer on the page.
3. **No founder/company identity** — "Who are you?" is unanswered. For a free product, this triggers "what are they really selling?" suspicion.
4. **"Free forever" explanation comes too late** — the business model should be transparent by Section 2, not Section 7.
5. **Two conversion paths create friction** — the page should sell the free signup and let the product sell the upgrade.
6. **The "1 in 5" stat is overused and undersourced** — lean on it less or source it more visibly.
7. **Missing critical objections** — lowball estimates fear, same-market competition, company sustainability.
8. **Section 5 (Proof) is the weakest section** — "see the quality" sells the product instead of the outcome.
9. **Inconsistency between "2 minutes" (this draft) and "5 minutes" (research doc)** — pick one, verify it, commit.
10. **"Your reviews and SEO stop working"** is an overstatement that could damage credibility.

### If I had to prioritize three fixes:

1. **Rewrite the hero** to lead with outcome, not a question.
2. **Add social proof** — even industry stats framed as proof, or a live demo with visible leads/results.
3. **Move the "why it's free" explanation to immediately after the hero** — defuse the biggest objection before anything else.

### Bottom line:

The copy is a solid B-minus first draft. The research behind it is strong — the pain points, competitive positioning, and Google filter angle are all well-chosen. The execution just needs to be sharper. The page currently EXPLAINS the product well but doesn't SELL the outcome hard enough. A roofer should finish reading this page and think "I'm losing money every day I don't have this." Right now, they'd think "huh, that seems like a good deal." Good deals get bookmarked. Urgency gets signups.
